Individuals want input into the decisions their society makes.

What if individuals were economically incentivized for exerting this decision making control...but only if it was in alignment with the subjective wishes of the wider societal group?

Distributed Courts combine two potent elements:
1) The ability for individuals to proportionally control all the decision making assigned to the dCourts Function in a Society Protocol instance.

2) The ability for participants to economically benefit from 
decision making which aligns with the wishes of the 
wider societal group. ^nHjBb0mw

dCourts give us the ability to make granular decisions,
without involving everyone, while still retaining the general will and sentiment of our society. 

dCourts are a risk Value Function, which mimic courts and juries in real life––a random sampling of citizens who are all slightly incentivized to make the best decision to improve their societies with varying subjective viewpoints.

What dCourts add, is: Jury distribution, economic incentives, and participation from only the subset of society who wants to participate (by staking their Energy to the dCourts Function).

This Jury Pool offloads the cost from the wider society by playing an incentivized game with each other (they risk Energy and it ultimately ends up with the winning Jurors)––forming a consensus opinion in the process. Additionally, Jurors profit from the Proposal Cost paid by Actors and dCorps who are wanting to initiate Cases. 

Distributed Courts can control any of the 5 Governance Domains granted to them. Varying dCourts settings of: Jury sizes, Hooks, variants of decision making, and thresholds for resolution can be used for each Governance Domain and the sectors within them separately.

An important issue of cartel formation in side-channels is resolved by: 1) Randomizing the Jury selected for each case from the Jury Pool.
2) Double-checking the Jury pool against the wider population or another subset at random intervals (or under specific conditions in the Appeal to the Masses Hook) with economic penalties for differentiation. 

Over time, this system incentivizes our Jurors to become empathetic telepaths––voting not what their individual opinion is, but rather what they perceive to become the most likely subjective consensus outcome for the Jury. 

Out of our two Governance Functions, dCourts are meant to surface and resolve in subjective consensus opinions––rather than individualist ones. Meaning, dCourts do not judge whether decisions are right or wrong, only that they are the subjective consensus of the group. ^m4rmykLS

Terminology ^3TuOlsUZ

Court Participation Cost Unit (CPCU) – The Energy cost for one spot in the Jury Pool.
Jury Pool – The total pool of participants who paid the CPCU at the dCourts Function.
Jury – The randomized subset of the Jury Pool assigned to a specific Case.
Juror – An Individual Actor or dCorp participating in dCourts.
Proposal Cost – The Energy cost paid for bringing up a proposal or dispute to the dCourt. 
Case – A specific decision making request which has been brought to the dCourt by paying the Proposal Cost. ^9Uwz0px6

This process will likely form into political parties, there is no way to stop this aggregation into parties––as people don't behave or exist as islands. What's important isn't the inevitable formation of political parties, it's that these political cartel or cabal forces don't go against the wishes of the public and extract value at their expense. 

It's important to keep the Governance (Distributed Courts) Function encapsulated into legitimate battles of Governors, rather than a degradation, where one or a few parties, controls the dCourts system and extracts value from the entire Society Protocol instance without recourse. 

This is accomplished via Hooks: Optimistic Governance, Backup Majority, and Emergency Transfer.  ^yBWTk1aY

Society requires the ability to make human decisions as a group, in both microscopic and macro scenarios––when the protocols algorithms aren't enough or capable to resolve the issue––somebody has to steer the ship.

What we want to accomplish as a society is: good group decision-making. This conceptually requires two characteristics:
1) Not force everyone into decisions. (It's incredibly inefficient, 
most people are apathetic to most decisions, and most do not have the
resources or information to make good decisions in most cases).
2) The societal interest is to create a meritocracy of good decisions, 
while avoiding the devolution into governance cartels and cabals, 
which extract value from the public without a backstop 
on their decision-making power. ^BuMOgp89

You make bad decisions. You aren't special; everyone else is just like you––you all make bad majority decisions as a group. Together!

You're constantly winning Energy from the other Jurors by being in the majority, but that Energy is ever becoming more worthless with each and every decision––correlating with the value of your society.  ^6qT1vxWc

You are part of a dominant political party which makes good decisions and outmanuevers political rivals to be the winners (majority) in Cases often. 

You are improving your society with good policy which slightly aligns with your interests, make Energy off your majority decisions, and that Energy is worth more than ever due to your societies thriving conditions. ^UnDDcMuF

You choose to participate in the Governance, in the
dCourts, because you want to improve your society through contributions and gather some Activity Score with your idle Energy. 

It turns out you're pretty good. While you don't make millions, your contributions help tie-break choices and improve your Society Protocol instance––making the Energy inside it worth more for everyone. You have also gained a lot of Activity Score, because your instance values dCourt participation metrics a lot. ^Z8aqudqM

    - What Governance Domains do the Decentralized Courts control?
    - What Variants of Voting do they use to come to a decision? 
    - What percentage of the Jury Pool is selected for each Case? 
    - Who exactly gets to select a list of options? 
    - What's the threshold for resolution? 
    - What percentage of the time does the Optimistic Governance Hook 
trigger?

What is the scale of punishment, in the case where the Optimistic Governance, Backup Majority, or Emergency Transfer mechanism invalidates the Jury's decision (the range of this can exceed the Energy staked into the dCourts Function). Slashed Jurors could lose the ability to participate in the Jury Pool again for a certain amount of time, or even Death in a very extreme variant of Society Protocol).

Do the winning Jurors take Energy from the losing Jurors or only win the Proposal Cost for the case?

Do Actors and dCorps need to exist in the system for a certain amount of time before participating in the Distributed Courts Function, what are the parameters for participation? (This would be an infraction against Equal Rights in our ideology, but could be desirable in some societies.)

For streaming voting processes the dCourt system could spawn some form of ephermal structure, like an Ephermal DAO (or dCorp). This mechanism values purpose over permanence, once the issue is resolved the Jury and Ephermal DAO dissolves. How would this work?

A regional system of many Distributed Courts (like state and regional governance) to hold courts and make decisions at a more granular local level. (It's actually often/sometimes preferable to have a global system, in order to get a more objectively fair judgement.) How would this work? 

Delegation can be possible. Jurors can solicit Energy from other Actors and Organizations, as an investment to participate in the risk activity of dCourts. ^JMExGnGZ

Individual Actors can allocate their Energy, in Court Participation Cost Units (CPCUs), from their Reserve Energy into the Distributed Courts Function––forming a Jury Pool of Energy. ^ofeb8Eke

When an Actor or Organization desires a dCourt Case to change something in the protocol or resolve a dispute: they pay the Proposal Cost, and a new Case is opened for them. A randomized subset of the Jury Pool is selected to resolve it as a Jury. 

The Jury participants (+ potentially some false Jury participants to add randomness) CPCUs go into a lock (attributable to them). ^feekZhzr

There are a few ways the Jury can select the resolution options for the Case. One way is: everyone is able to include one or many resolution options. Another is a randomized subset from the Jury can be selected to create a list of resolution options (and the accompanying code).  ^Axo8u6OS

All hooks enabled on the dCourts become active. There is one mandatory Hook on the voting, which is either Optimistic Governance, Backup Majority, or Emergency Transfer. Optimistic Governance is a Hook which double-checks the Jury's votes against, either a subset, or the entire population of the Society Protocol instance at randomized intervals––overwriting the Jury if they differ (and punishing the Jury if the majority of the Optimistic Governance population disagrees by slashing their CPCUs). Backup Majority and Emergency Transfer Hooks function similarly, but trigger under different conditions. ^WlwarOdr

7. Settlement ^9YCVUpOx

A Darkpool is created which grants everyone in the Jury empty encrypted voting notes. Every Jury member must submit their vote for one of the choices available. (This may differ slightly in accordance with which variants of decision making are used in dCourts and in this particular Case.)  ^kPVsXarD

The Jury comes to a decision. That decision is automatically implemented into the protocol codebase. The subjective parts of the Jury's decision are recorded in the public state.  ^E7uuiahN

All the Jury members who voted in the winning options––split the Proposal Cost paid to the dCourt along with the CPCUs from the Jurors who voted against the winning options.  ^5kBo3Uty

### Zero-Entry Accounting Age (ZEAA) – Tribes (Pre 10,000 BCE – 3,500 BCE)

Humans lived in small bands of 20–50 people during this period, until the Neolithic Revolution around 10,000 BCE—when agriculture enabled larger communities. Afterwards, early farming villages formed, but their total populations didn’t scale much past _Dunbars number (around 150 people)_ during this epoch. _Just small bands and tribes._

Identity was held in place entirely in the minds of the members of the tribe (reputation). **Reputation** **was everything** (it still is today but fortunately it’s not held solely in the malleable minds of our neighbors). Life, death, and roles depended entirely on how those 20–150 malleable minds felt about any individual during any given moment.

There was no way to permanently record any state, and no way to survive alone as an individual. Without your identity, which existed in the minds of your tribal members—you’re ☠️. That’s your ancient passport, it’s your reputation. It didn’t interoperate much—other tribes were mostly hostile and had no reputation or identity for you = ☠️☠️.

#### Roles

**_Identity was more fluid._** Roles could change rapidly due to circumstances and were created or changed using _rituals_.

Identity shifts were centered around rituals: the ritual of birth, the ritual of becoming a man, the ritual of becoming a mother, the ritual of becoming a hunter, the ritual of becoming an elder, the ritual of dying. _The rituals and confessions around a fire could shift fluid identity roles._

The specialization of roles was fluid and there wasn’t too much hierarchy. There was no massive State holding your role in position or trying to tax and identify you, just 20–150 people tribes, who communally arrived at a fluid consensus.

People weren’t practicing their whole lives or bloodlines to become scribes, lawyers, or doctors. An individuals past could easily be erased and adjusted. All that was necessary, was to convince the malleable minds of the members of their tribe.

The legacies of these rituals weren’t recorded. Everything during the ZEAA was mired in impermanence[^5]. There was no paperwork or documents holding identities in place, _only reputation_.

> Identities were vivid flames, not frozen files.

Individuals had to fit their “role” into the tribal State’s shared reality of social cohesion (a small range). Their only freedom to differentiate individually was inside that tiny range. Lots of death outside that range.

During the ZEAA, individuals whole concept of lasting identity probably wasn’t as deep or strategic as ours today. They probably didn’t ponder about who they were so much, because it constantly changed, but rather acted on it—locked into whatever their identity was in the moment (locked into that level of Maslow’s hierarchy of needs).

Roles were fully attached to ethnicity, your Tribe (State) _<u>was</u>_ your ethnicity.

#### Privacy

While privacy from the tribe was rare; privacy over time was common. Whatever an individual did, could easily be wiped from the record, _because there was no record_. No CCTV cameras, no credit scores…Individuals were tracked by grandmothers. 

#### Individualism

Identity was communal property. Individualism was suppressed. Isolation was reduced in small bands, and the social pressure to conform was amplified.

The shared reality heavily controlled identity—_limiting individualism and specialization to only the identities the tribe required at any given moment_. The tribal State’s small margin for bare survival didn’t allow it to invest in individualism, anyone standing out as too eccentric faced ostracism.

The social cohesion (reputation system) was heavily influenced by the tribal elders—who had outsized control and influence over the consensus of the shared reality. This bred humility, but limited self-actualization.

#### Coordination

Tribes couldn’t coordinate well during the ZEAA. They were unable to create specialized individualistic individuals (who would facilitate greater coordination) or record state to help them coordinate. As a result, tribal societies during the ZEAA couldn’t scale.

#### Agency

To update their identity, individuals had to act on, and convince the malleable minds of the members of their tribe (including their own mind). There was nothing else holding identities in place. _No need to go to divorce court and dissolve that marriage certificate._

#### Summary & Limitations of the ZEAA

- Knowledge couldn’t spread, the death of elder knowledge holders meant setting the entire tribe back. _Knowledge wasn’t separated from the individual._
- Identities were constrained to a really small box (they were fluid but in a really narrow range).
- Identities couldn’t specialize or become individualistic (the tribe didn’t have enough R&D resources).
- Property rights weren’t able to be recorded, and neither was language. Information couldn’t spread.
- As a result, States couldn’t scale (empires only emerged during the SEAA).

### Single-Entry Accounting Age (SEAA) – Classical States (3500 BCE–476 CE)

Written records enabled the accumulation of knowledge, bureaucratic control, and cultural preservation. This fundamentally altered identity from fluid collective orality…to documented hierarchical forms. It transformed identity into something archivable and verifiable, shifting the emphasis from memory to inscribed records.

Hierarchies emerged. Classical States imposed documented identities for censuses, taxes, and military conscription. This boosted coordination amongst the elites while simultaneously eroding power, privacy, and the tribal bonds from the vast majority of people within the societal pyramid—who couldn’t access or alter the recorded state.

Knowledge became separated from identities for the first time in history. During the ZEAA, _identity was synonymous with knowledge_. “Elder loss” meant losing all the knowledge stored within that person. It was only when written records emerged that identity and knowledge became separated.

Identity was still intimately tied to reputation and the opinions of the close ones around you, but it was also inscribed by the scribes and communicated up the pyramid to the rulers and their representatives in a macro sense, as rough macro mappings. Identity became tied, to entire empires, through records.

#### Roles

Identity became role-based and codified (e.g., "citizen", “pharaoh”, or "slave"), enhancing efficiency but stifling improvisation. _Fluidity decreased in favor of specialization._

Society acquired and recorded specialized roles, ranks, and skills (such as “tax collector”, “scribe”, or “legionnaire”), but it was not optimal or meritocratic in the ways it prescribed individuals their roles. Societies’ talent-finding mechanisms remained dull until the double-entry accounting age.

Identity became less ephemeral and more permanent, it became more engrained in people. This enabled enduring legacies and changed people to consider even the future identity roles of their descendants.

Roles were still heavily ethnically based, but interoperable: Greek city-states like Athens often defined citizenship through descent, emphasizing Athenian lineage. Foreigners were excluded from full political rights, often taken as slaves, and myths of common ancestry were used to reinforce social cohesion. _Yet,_ _Classical States had evolved beyond the single-ethnicity nature of Tribes._

#### Individualism

_It wasn’t possible to have a difference of opinion safely from the ruling class._ Individuals at the top retained a deep sense of individuality and uniqueness while those who couldn't store or record the state became commoditized as peasants and slaves. Slavery was documented, commodifying people who couldn't be differentiated with a specialized unique role. You could say the elites “[commoditized their compliments](https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2002/06/12/strategy-letter-v/).”

The SEAA enabled very rough economic tracking. Assets were able to be inscribed and identities became able to accumulate lasting property, tying an individual’s worth to recorded property for the first time .

#### Privacy

Privacy decreased significantly; records like tax ledgers and censuses (e.g., Roman census every 5 years) created State surveillance, tracking names, property, and debts. No more “forgetting” mistakes at the campfire—debts or crimes were permanently inscribed.

_Still, this tracking wasn’t very precise._

#### Coordination

Coordination vastly improved; bureaucracies managed large-scale projects (e.g., pyramids, aqueducts) via written orders and accounts. Single-entry ledgers tracked resources efficiently, enabling armies of 100,000+ (e.g., Persian or Roman).

Empires could coordinate massive endeavors with more specialized individualistic roles at the top and many commoditized undifferentiated roles (peasants and slaves) at the bottom and middle of the societal pyramid.

This enabled society to build large lumbering empires, but they ultimately weren’t too coordinated. They could use rough macro coordination from the top, but had trouble coordinating horizontally, and tracking & transmitting precise micro details.

#### Agency

To change identity became more difficult, as it now required changing this hierarchical structure (in the shared reality mentally) and changing the inscribed recordings of it.

#### Summary & Limitations of the SEAA

- During the SEAA massive lumbering hierarchical empire structure formed. They swallowed up tribes as empires swelled in size—absorbing tribes into their shared reality. Bureaucracies emerged, and society became able to manage large scale projects.
- Identity became more rigid and specialized. Formal roles were recorded and became difficult to alter.
- Knowledge became separated from the source individual. Records of property emerged.
- It was limited by the State’s inability to distribute the allocations of specialized roles in meritocratic ways.
- Limited in its inability to scale identity records horizontally (only a vertical integration to the king/pharaoh).
- Limited by the State’s inability to handle individualism (lumbering empires couldn’t handle much differentiation or disagreement with the rulers while maintaining social cohesion).

### Double-Entry Accounting Age (DEAA) – Nation States (500 – Present)

The foundation of the DEAA is duplicated contracts (humans learned to duplicate the state), which enabled democratic principles. Identity became held in place by three layers:

1. **Local:** **Reputation** (from the ZEAA)
2. **Vertical:** **The State’s permanent written records** (from the SEAA)
3. **Horizontal:** **Duplicated contracts shared between “equal” parties** (DEAA)

Duplicated contracts hold identity in-place during the DEAA—both vertically (between the individual and State, such as a birth certificate) and horizontally (between multiple parties considered as “equals”, such as a social media _like_).

The DEAA made people “accountable to each other.” Duplicated contracts have allowed our identities and reputations to become globally scaleable, interoperable, and even interactive (you need to like this post to interact with my identity).

During this epoch we saw a shift from collective feudal obligations to individualism after the Print Revolution democratized access to CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete) the recorded state—_allowing individuals to create bespoke duplicated contracts between themselves_. This evolution changed the nature of States from Ecclesiastical States to Nation States while facilitating a Cambrian explosion of information during the Renaissance, Scientific Revolution, and Enlightenment. **This changed everything.** Knowledge became widespread and decoupled from identity. Easy access to this duplicated information made every individual in society more intelligent and individualistic.

This breakthrough was unlocked by democratic principles. Until the advent of democratic governance principles, during the SEAA, Classical States were unable to keep social cohesion with a plurality of perspectives which differed from the rulers’ perspective. Democratic principles allowed multiple parties, sharing a State, to disagree about certain topics while still coordinating as one structure and maintaining social cohesion.

### Conclusion

**What makes us who we are?** The fundamental architecture of our identities—every role we can inhabit, every dream we're permitted to pursue, every boundary of self-expression—is ultimately determined by the State structures surrounding us.

More advanced State structures can afford greater specialization (allowing unique talents to flourish), expanded individualism (diverse expressions of self), enhanced agency (more potential for action), superior coordination (enabling complex collective achievements), and stronger social cohesion (maintaining unity amid diversity).

**This creates a paradox at the heart of human development**: We cannot transcend our individual limitations without collectively transcending our societal limitations. **The deeper truth:** Individual self-actualization is not only a personal project—it's a civilizational one. We can't become better people without building better societies. _We are merely a reflection of the universe around us._

At each stage of societal evolution, identities have changed along with the State structures. The overall pattern is:

- **Roles** have become more specialized and better selected
- **Individualism** always trends up and expands
- **Privacy** always decreases, but also changes in nature
- **Societal coordination** always increases

**During the ZEAA**: Identities were fluid and entirely based on whatever dualistic beliefs of the tribe around them had at the current moment. _Whatever the tribe needed in an unpredictable, uncharted, and unstable world at the present moment._

**During the SEAA**: Identities were based directly on individuals proximity to the vertical integrations of recorded state. The further from the records—the more commoditized and duller the individual; the closer to the records—the more individualistic and intelligent.

**During the DEAA**: Identities are based on duplicated contracts and democratic principles. The ideals of _equality underpin both, facilitating horizontal interactions,_ and empowering the middle-men who facilitate them.

- The worldwide Synchronized Social Contract (SSC) will make everyone more connected, aware, and intelligent—able to make better decisions as a society at scale.

- _The state of the present moment and history will become more illuminated._ 

_We are also certain many changes we do not foresee are coming, and will alter identity in drastic and profound ways._

The Age of Synchronicity awaits us, and it will be as big of a shift for humanity as learning to inscribe permanent records in the SEAA, and the duplicated contracts fostering democratic principles of equality in the DEAA.
